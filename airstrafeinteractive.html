Game Feel Pipeline

A detailed look into my process of adding a screenshake when a tree topples onto the ground.

Initial tree falling, pre-screenshake

Step 1: Identifying Opportunities for Polish

I regularly scan gameplay for areas that feel flat or could benefit from stronger feedback. One moment that stood out was chopping down a tree: a long buildup of swings with a payoff that felt underwhelming. To reward the player’s effort, I decided to add a satisfying screen shake when the tree hits the ground.

Dot product solution

Step 2: Early Testing & Problem Solving

My first approach was to trigger a screen shake when the tree's collider touched the ground. However, this fired too early; the base hit first, while the top was still mid-fall.
To solve this, I needed a way to detect when the entire tree had finished falling. I focused on detecting collisions specifically from the top half of the tree.

Code implementation (pre-serialization of screen shake values)

Step 3: Smart Collision Detection

I tested different methods and landed on a solution using dot products. By comparing the tree’s upward vector with the vector from its center to the collision point, I could check if the hit occurred on the top half (dot product > 0). This made the shake fire at just the right moment.

Final tree fall with screenshake

Step 4: Final Implementation & Tweaking

I triggered the effect through my ScreenShakeManager, using DOTween’s ShakePosition. A simple boolean ensured it only triggered once per fall. All shake values were exposed in the Inspector for quick tuning. The result? A consistent, juicy screen shake no matter the tree’s fall direction, turning a small moment into a memorable one.
